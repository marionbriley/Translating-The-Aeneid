Dido and Anna: English

679. Her sister heard. Half in a swoon, she ran
680. with trembling steps, where thickest was the throng ,
681. beating her breast, while with a desperate hand
682. she tore at her own face, and called aloud
683. upon the dying Queen.
684. “Was it for this
685. my own true sister used me with such guile?
686. O, was this horrid deed the dire intent
687. of altars, Iofty couch, and funeral fires?
688. What shall I tell for chiefest of my woes?
689. Lost that I am! Why, though in death, cast off
690. thy sister from thy heart? Why not invite
691. one mortal stroke for both, a single sword,
692. one agony together? But these hands
693. built up thy pyre; and my voice implored
694. the blessing of our gods, who granted me
695. that thou shouldst perish thus—and I not know!
696. In thy self-slaughter, sister, thou hast slain
697. myself, thy people, the grave counsellors
698. of Sidon, and yon city thou didst build
699. to be thy throne!—Go, fetch me water, there!
700. That I may bathe those gashes! If there be
701. one hovering breath that stays, let my fond lips
702. discover and receive!” So saying, she sprang up
703. from stair to stair, and, clasping to her breast
704. her sister's dying form, moaned grievously,
705. and staunched the dark blood with her garment's fold.
706. Vainly would Dido lift her sinking eyes,
707. but backward fell, while at her heart the wound
708. opened afresh; three times with straining arm
709. she rose; three times dropped helpless, her dimmed eyes
710. turned skyward, seeking the sweet light of day, —
711. which when she saw, she groaned.
712. Great Juno then
713. looked down in mercy on that lingering pain
714. and labor to depart: from realms divine
715. she sent the goddess of the rainbow wing,
716. Iris, to set the struggling spirit free
717. and loose its fleshly coil. For since the end
718. came not by destiny, nor was the doom
719. of guilty deed, but of a hapless wight
720. to sudden madness stung, ere ripe to die,
721. therefore the Queen of Hades had not shorn
722. the fair tress from her forehead, nor assigned
723. that soul to Stygian dark. So Iris came
724. on dewy, saffron pinions down from heaven,
725. a thousand colors on her radiant way,
726. from the opposing sun. She stayed her flight
727. above that pallid brow: “I come with power
728. to make this gift to Death. I set thee free
729. from thy frail body's bound.” With her right hand
730. she cut the tress: then through its every limb
731. the sinking form grew cold; the vital breath
732. fled forth, departing on the viewless air.
